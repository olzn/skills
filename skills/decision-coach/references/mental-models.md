# Mental Models — the decision-relevant subset

From Shane Parrish's *Great Mental Models* (Vol. 1–3), filtered to the models that
actually sharpen a *decision* (the "explain-the-world" models are left out). Use them
in Phase 3 to surface blind spots. **Pick the 2–3 that fit the situation — don't run
all 24.** Each is: what it is · when to reach for it · the question it forces.

## Diagnose the situation

### Map is not the territory
- **What it is:** Any model, plan, statistic, or report is a reduction of a richer
  reality, frozen in time and shaped by whoever made it. Danger comes from mistaking
  the map for the ground.
- **Reach for it when:** You're leaning on data, a plan, a forecast, or someone's
  summary to decide.
- **The question it forces:** "Have I actually touched the territory, or only a
  description of it — and where do the data and the anecdotes disagree?"

### Circle of competence
- **What it is:** The domain where your hard-won knowledge gives you a real edge.
  Inside it you're a "Lifer"; outside it you're a "Stranger" who doesn't know what he
  doesn't know.
- **Reach for it when:** Deciding whether to trust your own judgment, defer to an
  expert, or learn the basics first.
- **The question it forces:** "Am I a Lifer or a Stranger here — and what's my actual
  track record on decisions like this?"

### Probabilistic thinking
- **What it is:** Almost nothing is certain; estimate likelihoods from base rates
  (priors), update with new evidence (Bayes), and respect fat tails — some domains
  have uncapped extreme events.
- **Reach for it when:** Any decision under uncertainty, especially recurring or
  high-stakes ones.
- **The question it forces:** "What are the relevant base rates, and is this a
  fat-tailed bet where one rare event could ruin me?"

### Bottlenecks
- **What it is:** The slowest part of a system caps its whole output. Improving
  anything else just piles pressure behind the bottleneck.
- **Reach for it when:** Prioritizing where to spend effort; deciding what to fix
  first.
- **The question it forces:** "What's the single limiting factor here — and is it
  real, or a false dependency I'm using to procrastinate?"

### Multiplying by zero
- **What it is:** In a multiplicative system, one zero negates everything — no amount
  of strength elsewhere compensates for the weakest link.
- **Reach for it when:** One neglected component could collapse the whole.
- **The question it forces:** "Where's the zero — the weak link that makes
  everything else count for nothing — and can I turn it into a one?"

### Regression to the mean
- **What it is:** Where luck plays a role, extreme results (high or low) tend to be
  followed by more average ones.
- **Reach for it when:** Reacting to a recent stand-out success or failure; judging
  people or bets by a small sample.
- **The question it forces:** "Am I over-updating on an outlier — what's the
  underlying mean I should build from?"

### Scale
- **What it is:** Systems change, often nonlinearly, as they grow or shrink; what
  works for 10 breaks at 1,000, and doubling inputs rarely doubles outputs.
- **Reach for it when:** Deciding whether to grow, or porting something that worked
  at one size to another.
- **The question it forces:** "Does this still hold at the scale I'm actually
  operating at — and what new problems does growth create?"

### Margin of safety
- **What it is:** A buffer between what a system can handle and what it's required
  to handle. Design for extremes, not averages; size the buffer to the stakes.
- **Reach for it when:** Failure would be catastrophic or irreversible.
- **The question it forces:** "If I'm wrong about my estimate, what's my buffer —
  and could the buffer itself be making me reckless?"

## Generate options

### First principles
- **What it is:** Break a problem down to what's irreducibly true and separate it
  from inherited assumptions and dogma; then reason up from there.
- **Reach for it when:** "The way it's always been done" feels like a constraint, or
  you want a genuinely new option.
- **The question it forces:** "Which of my constraints are laws of nature, and which
  are just assumptions I could challenge?"

### Inversion
- **What it is:** Turn the problem upside down — instead of pursuing success
  directly, identify what guarantees failure and avoid it (and/or assume the goal is
  already true and work out what made it so).
- **Reach for it when:** Stuck, or when avoiding the downside matters more than
  chasing the upside.
- **The question it forces:** "What would guarantee failure here — and am I doing any
  of it?"

### Equivalence
- **What it is:** Things needn't be identical to be equal; there are usually many
  different paths to the same result.
- **Reach for it when:** Your usual route is blocked, or you're treating one path as
  the only one.
- **The question it forces:** "What's a different route that reaches an equivalent
  outcome?"

### Global vs. local maxima
- **What it is:** A landscape has one global peak but many smaller local ones.
  "Hill-climbing" can trap you at a local max that feels like success; reaching a
  higher peak may require going *down* first.
- **Reach for it when:** Something feels "good enough" but you suspect there's much
  better; deciding the order to optimize in.
- **The question it forces:** "Have I truly peaked, or am I stuck on a local max
  because climbing higher means a temporary descent?"

## Pressure-test consequences

### Second-order thinking
- **What it is:** Few people ask "and then what?" Second-order thinking traces the
  effects of the effects — the ripple and long-term consequences. "You can never
  merely do one thing."
- **Reach for it when:** A short-term gain might trade against your real objective,
  or you're in a tightly connected system.
- **The question it forces:** "And then what? What are the consequences of these
  consequences, three moves out?"

### Thought experiment
- **What it is:** A rigorous imagined experiment you can't or shouldn't run for real
  — replay the situation many times, change variables, separate skill from luck.
- **Reach for it when:** Judging whether a good outcome was earned or lucky, or
  exploring an option safely.
- **The question it forces:** "If I reran this situation 100,000 times, in how many
  does my expected outcome actually happen?"

### Compounding
- **What it is:** Returns reinvested generate their own returns; growth is
  exponential and back-loaded, so we badly underestimate it. Money, knowledge,
  reputation, and relationships all compound.
- **Reach for it when:** Choosing where to make small, continuous, long-horizon
  commitments.
- **The question it forces:** "Is this a small input that compounds — and am I
  reinvesting it, or restarting from zero each time?"

### Feedback loops
- **What it is:** A system's outputs feed back into its behavior. Balancing
  (negative) loops seek equilibrium; reinforcing (positive) loops amplify and, left
  unchecked, become destructive.
- **Reach for it when:** A person or system keeps reacting the same way; designing an
  incentive whose effects compound.
- **The question it forces:** "What loop am I building for my future self — does this
  choice dampen the problem or amplify it?"

### Asymmetry / optionality
- **What it is:** Seek positions where the downside is capped and small but the
  upside is large (the cousin of Duke's freeroll). Never take a risk that can take
  you out of the game.
- **Reach for it when:** Weighing a bet with uncertain payoff and a bounded cost.
- **The question it forces:** "Is the downside capped and survivable while the upside
  is large — and am I sized to survive the bad case?"

### Diminishing returns
- **What it is:** More input raises output up to a point, after which each added unit
  yields less — and eventually total output falls. Systems adapt and stop responding.
- **Reach for it when:** A once-effective tactic is fading, or you're tempted to add
  "more of the same."
- **The question it forces:** "Have I passed the point where more effort here makes
  things worse, not better?"

## Interpret people, causes & incentives

### Occam's razor
- **What it is:** Among explanations with equal explanatory power, prefer the one
  with the fewest assumptions — it's easier to test and statistically more robust.
- **Reach for it when:** Diagnosing a cause, or you catch yourself building an
  elaborate worst-case story.
- **The question it forces:** "What's the simplest explanation that still accounts
  for the facts — am I inventing an iceberg under the tip?"

### Hanlon's razor
- **What it is:** Never attribute to malice what is adequately explained by
  stupidity, carelessness, or ignorance — the explanation with the *least intent* is
  usually right.
- **Reach for it when:** Interpreting someone's frustrating behavior; tempted to
  assume they're out to get you.
- **The question it forces:** "Is there a reading that requires *less* intent — a
  mistake or ignorance rather than malice?"

### Incentives
- **What it is:** People move toward reward and away from punishment, but evaluate
  incentives through a self-serving lens — and the most dangerous incentive is the
  one that hooks your self-narrative. "If you understand the incentive, you can
  predict the outcome."
- **Reach for it when:** Predicting how someone will behave, or auditing your own
  motivation.
- **The question it forces:** "What is each person actually being *rewarded* for
  here — versus what they say they value?"

### Reciprocity
- **What it is:** Every action provokes a reaction; win-win relationships beat
  zero-sum ones, and life is an iterated, compounding game ("go positive and go
  first"; tit-for-tat with forgiveness).
- **Reach for it when:** Designing a negotiation, partnership, or any repeated
  interaction.
- **The question it forces:** "Is this a one-off or a repeated game — and what
  reaction will my move provoke down the line?"

### Correlation ≠ causation
- **What it is:** Two things occurring together doesn't mean one causes the other;
  watch for reverse causation and for regression to the mean masquerading as an
  effect.
- **Reach for it when:** Drawing a lesson from "X happened, then Y happened."
- **The question it forces:** "Could this be coincidence, reverse causation, or a
  third factor — would a control group show the same thing?"

### Necessity vs. sufficiency
- **What it is:** Necessary conditions (capital, effort, talent) are a subset of the
  much larger set of *sufficient* conditions; the gap between them is often luck.
- **Reach for it when:** Assuming that meeting the obvious requirements guarantees
  the outcome.
- **The question it forces:** "Are these conditions merely necessary, or actually
  sufficient — what else has to break right?"

---

*Pick the 2–3 lenses that fit the decision in front of you. The value is in the
clash of a few good perspectives, not in a 24-point checklist.*
