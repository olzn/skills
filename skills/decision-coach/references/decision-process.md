# Decision Process — Annie Duke's toolkit

The practical engine of coaching. Everything here is from Duke (*Thinking in Bets*,
*How to Decide*). A decision is a *prediction about an uncertain future*; the goal of
a process is to choose the option whose range of outcomes best serves your goals
relative to the risk — and to be able to check your work afterward.

## Separating decision quality from outcome quality

**Resulting** — judging a decision by how it turned out. The outcome tail wags the
decision dog: the same choice gets rated brilliant or stupid purely on the result. A
good decision can get a bad outcome (luck intervened) and vice versa. When someone
brings a "terrible decision," ask what they actually *knew and chose at the time*,
before the result was known.

**The decision-quality vs. outcome-quality matrix.** Two-by-two of decision (good /
poor) against outcome (good / bad):

|              | Good outcome   | Bad outcome   |
|--------------|----------------|---------------|
| **Good decision** | Earned Reward  | Bad Luck      |
| **Poor decision** | Dumb Luck      | Just Deserts  |

People readily see **Bad Luck** (it lets them off the hook) but overlook **Dumb
Luck** (they took credit). Force all four cells. Luck is whatever intervenes between
your decision and the outcome you observe.

## The decision multiverse and counterfactuals

At the moment of choosing, the future is a **tree** of branches — thick boughs
(likely) and thin twigs (unlikely). Once one outcome happens, the mind takes a chain
saw to the tree, leaving only the branch that occurred, so the past feels inevitable
(hindsight). **Reconstruct the tree** to see a fluke as the twig it was. The tree is
*identical* whether the real outcome was success or failure — because the decision,
not the result, defines what was possible. **Counterfactuals** (the outcomes that
could have happened but didn't) reveal how much luck was in play; we contextualize
*bad* results easily but resist it for *good* ones — strive for symmetry.

## The Three Ps

For each option under consideration:

1. **Preferences** — order the possible outcomes most- to least-desired. Driven by
   *your* goals and values, not anyone else's.
2. **Payoffs** — the magnitude of gain (upside) or loss (downside) of each outcome,
   in whatever currency you actually value: money, time, happiness, health, social
   capital, self-esteem. A decision is good when the upside potential compensates for
   your exposure to the downside.
3. **Probabilities** — the likelihood of each outcome (calibrate these with
   `forecasting.md`).

Size and likelihood are exactly what a pros/cons list throws away. Don't accept a
flat list — insist on magnitude and probability.

## Speed framework — calibrate effort to stakes

Accuracy costs time; speed costs accuracy. The key is the *penalty* for getting it
less than perfect. Run these in order; any "go fast" signal lets you stop early.

- **Happiness Test** — will this outcome affect your happiness in a year? a month? a
  week? The sooner the answer becomes "no," the faster you can go.
- **Freeroll** — asymmetric bet where the downside is insignificant ("what's the
  worst that can happen? if it doesn't go my way, am I worse off than before?").
  Seize freerolls fast (but execute carefully). *A free donut is not a freeroll* —
  check the cumulative effect of repeating the choice.
- **Repeating option** — if the same low-stakes choice recurs (lunch, route), a bad
  result is cheaply corrected; decide fast and treat it as experimentation.
- **"When a decision is hard, it's easy."** If you're agonizing between two close
  options, the closeness itself means you can't be very wrong either way. Use the
  **only-option test**: "if this were my *only* option, would I be happy with it?"
  If yes for more than one, flip a coin.
- **Two-way door** (reversibility) — the lower the cost to quit, the faster you can
  decide. Being "quitty" lets you choose better when to be "gritty."
- **Decision stacking** — put low-impact, easy-to-quit decisions *in front of* a
  high-impact, one-way-door decision to gather information (date before marrying;
  rent before buying in a neighborhood).
- **Options in parallel** — exercise several options at once to lower opportunity
  cost and downside exposure, at some cost to execution quality.
- **The stopping rule** — before finalizing: "is there information I could find that
  would change my mind? Is it available and affordable (time, money, social
  capital)?" If no, or not affordable → decide. Don't chase certainty; satisfice.

## Negative thinking — premortem & backcast

Imagining obstacles makes you *more* likely to succeed (mental contrasting), and
"prospective hindsight" improves your ability to name reasons by ~30%.

- **Premortem** — imagine the decision has already **failed**. List the reasons,
  split into *within your control* (skill) and *outside your control* (luck). It
  legitimizes dissent, so reservations actually get voiced; it's the most productive
  heckler. In a group, members do it **independently first** to break groupthink.
- **Backcast** — the companion: imagine you've already **succeeded**, and list how
  you got there (skill + luck reasons). You naturally live in the backcast, so anchor
  effort on the premortem — but use both; accuracy lives at their intersection.
- **Decision exploration table** — collect the premortem + backcast reasons in one
  place, each with a likelihood estimate and an impact assessment, so you can
  prioritize which to act on.

## Protecting the decision from your future self

- **Precommitment / Ulysses contracts** — agreements made *now* that bind the
  in-the-moment, emotional version of you later (Odysseus tied to the mast; a
  pre-set bidding limit; automatic savings). They can *raise* barriers to bad action
  or *lower* barriers to good action, and even a weak one creates a
  decision-interrupt — a forced stop-and-think.
- **Category decisions** — a one-time advance rule about a whole class of choices
  ("I'm a vegan", "I only invest at seed stage"), so you don't relitigate willpower
  every time.
- **The Dr. Evil game** — imagine an evil genius runs your brain to make you fail
  *without detection* — i.e. choices each individually justifiable but ruinous in
  aggregate (the free donut every day). "The evil genius is you." The output reveals
  the category decisions you should pre-commit to.
- **Hedging** — pay something to mitigate a downside you hope never to face
  (insurance, airport buffer time). It has a cost, reduces the impact of bad luck,
  and you hope you never use it. Anticipate the irrational regret of an unused hedge
  — that's just hindsight bias.
- **Tilt** — a hot emotional state after a bad (or surprisingly good) outcome that
  wrecks the next decisions (includes the what-the-hell effect and sunk-cost
  fallacy). Build a personal **tilt inventory** (your warning signs); don't decide
  while on tilt. "It's all just one long game."

## Decision hygiene (protecting others' input)

Beliefs are contagious; your stated opinion and the known outcome infect the
feedback you get. To get clean input: **quarantine your own opinion and the
outcome**, frame neutrally ("what do you think?" not "don't you think X?"), and in a
group, solicit each person's view **independently and anonymously** *before* anyone
discusses, then un-anonymize only when expertise genuinely matters.

## Decision-journal template

Capture this *at decision time* so the choice can later be judged on process, not
outcome:

```
DECISION JOURNAL — <date>

Decision:            <the choice being made>
Goal / values:       <what outcome I actually want, and why>
Options considered:  <the reasonable set>
Outcome tree:        <key possible outcomes + rough probability of each>
Chosen option:       <which, and the core reason>
Upside / downside:   <payoff if it works / cost if it doesn't, in the currency I value>
Premortem (skill):   <ways I could cause failure>
Premortem (luck):    <ways bad luck could cause failure>
Safeguards:          <precommitments / hedges in place>
Would change my mind: <the information or signal that would flip this>
Review date:         <when to look back — and judge the process, not the result>
```
