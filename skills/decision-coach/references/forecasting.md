# Forecasting & Calibration — Philip Tetlock's toolkit

Used in Phase 2 (calibrating probabilities) and whenever a decision rests on a
belief about an uncertain future. From *Superforecasting*. The posture is
**"optimistic skeptic"**: real foresight exists in the messy middle, and forecasting
is a *skill* you improve by scoring yourself and updating — "beliefs are hypotheses
to be tested, not treasures to be guarded."

## Outside view → inside view

Start with the **base rate** *before* the case specifics. "How often do things of
this sort happen in situations of this sort?" Order matters because of **anchoring**
— we under-adjust from the first number we consider, so the first number should be a
meaningful one (the base rate), not the vivid details of this particular case. *Then*
take the **inside view**: adjust up or down for what's genuinely specific here,
investigated through hypotheses, not aimless reading. Accuracy lives at the
intersection.

## Fermi-izing (decomposition)

Break an intractable question into knowable and unknowable sub-questions (the classic
"how many piano tuners in Chicago?"). Ask **"what would have to be true for the answer
to be yes? for no?"** Flush your ignorance into the open; the errors in crude
sub-guesses tend to offset, producing surprisingly good estimates.

## Granular probabilities — numbers, not words

Vague verbiage ("serious possibility", "fair chance") means wildly different odds to
different people and lets a forecaster always seem right in hindsight. Translate
hunches into numbers, and use the **finest gradation the problem honestly supports**
(superforecasters routinely use single percentage points). For any individual
estimate:

- **Bull's-eye** — a point estimate.
- **Range** — an upper and lower bound signalling your uncertainty.
- **Shock test** — set the *narrowest* range where you'd still be genuinely shocked
  if the truth fell outside it; aim to capture the truth ~9 times in 10. A range of
  "0 to infinity" is useless; an overly narrow one oversells your knowledge (most
  people fail this test by being overconfident).

## Calibration vs. resolution, and the Brier score

- **Calibration** — when you say 70%, it happens ~70% of the time.
- **Resolution** — decisiveness: correctly pushing toward 0% and 100% instead of
  hovering in the 40–60 "shades of maybe" zone.
- **Brier score** — combines both (0 = perfect, 0.5 = chance, 2.0 = confidently and
  maximally wrong). Only meaningful against a **benchmark** ("always predict no
  change") and on a **level playing field** (same questions, same window).

## Updating in small increments

Most updates should be tiny "units of doubt" (e.g. 0.40 → 0.35), navigating between
two failure modes:

- **Under-reaction** — clinging to a belief because your ego/identity is invested in
  it (a low block in your Jenga tower topples others if pulled). Superforecasters
  invest little ego per forecast, so they revise freely.
- **Over-reaction** — the **dilution effect**: letting irrelevant information or
  noise swing a sound estimate.

Exception: when a forecast rests on a premise that has now collapsed, **rebuild from
scratch** rather than nudging.

## Fox vs. hedgehog

**Hedgehogs** know one Big Idea and force every problem through it — confident,
famous, and *less* accurate. **Foxes** are eclectic, gather many perspectives, talk
in "howevers" and "on the other hands," and aggregate (the "dragonfly eye"). Be a
fox. Get a "second opinion from yourself": assume your first answer is wrong and
argue why, then average.

## Active open-mindedness

Treat beliefs as hypotheses to be tested, not treasures to guard; value evidence and
people who disagree with you; changing your mind is strength. The operational
question: **"what would convince me I'm wrong?"**

## Scope sensitivity

People give the same answer whether 2,000 or 200,000 birds die, or whether a regime
might fall in 3 vs. 6 months — they answer "how bad does this feel?" instead of
scaling to magnitude/time. **Stress-test:** mentally vary the time frame or threshold
and check that your number actually moves.

## The wrong-side-of-maybe fallacy

Judging a "70% chance" forecast *wrong* simply because the event didn't happen. A
single outcome can't falsify a probability. Don't let this push you (or the person
you're coaching) toward vague, unfalsifiable language.

## The Ten Commandments (checklist)

1. **Triage** — focus on the Goldilocks zone; skip the trivially easy and the
   hopeless.
2. **Break** seemingly intractable problems into tractable sub-problems (Fermi-ize).
3. **Balance** the inside and outside views — find comparison classes even for
   "unique" events, then adjust.
4. **Balance** under- and over-reacting to evidence — update incrementally; hunt
   subtle lead indicators without being suckered by noise.
5. **Look for the clashing causal forces** at work — synthesize opposing views; list
   in advance what would change your mind.
6. **Distinguish as many degrees of doubt** as the problem permits — but no more.
7. **Balance** under- and over-confidence — score well on *both* calibration and
   resolution; avoid both waffling and blowhard certainty.
8. **Look for the errors behind your mistakes** — do unflinching postmortems on
   failures *and* successes, while guarding against rearview-mirror hindsight bias.
9. **Bring out the best in others** and let them bring out the best in you
   (perspective-taking, precision questioning, constructive confrontation).
10. **Master the error-balancing bicycle** — every commandment is a balance of
    opposing errors; you only learn it by doing, with clear feedback.
11. *(Bonus)* **Don't treat these as rigid commandments** — "two cases will never be
    exactly the same." Guidelines need mindfulness, not rote application.
